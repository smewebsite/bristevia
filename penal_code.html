PART 1

Article I. Time When the Act Takes Effect.

This Code shall take effect immediately upon the **Imperial Signature** of His Majesty the Souverion and Her Majesty the Souveria, and its promulgation throughout the Imperial Realm of Bristevia.

Article II. Application of Its Provisions.

Except as may be provided under treaties, compacts, or laws of preferential application, the provisions of this Code shall have force and effect throughout the entire **Dominion of Bristevia** — encompassing all provinces, dependencies, territories, and Imperial dominions, including its atmosphere, interior waters, and maritime jurisdiction.

This Code shall likewise apply beyond the Imperial boundaries to any person who:

  1. Commits an offense aboard an Imperial ship or airship registered under the flag of Bristevia;
  2. Forges or counterfeits any coin, currency, obligation, or security issued by the Imperial Treasury or any instrument of the Crown;
  3. Participates in the introduction or circulation of any such falsified obligations or securities within the Empire;
  4. Being an officer or servant of the Crown, commits an offense in the exercise of his official functions; or
  5. Commits crimes against the Imperial Crown, Imperial Security, or the Law of Nations, as defined in this Code.

TITLE ONE: Of Felonies and the Circumstances Which Affect Criminal Liability

CHAPTER ONE — Of Felonies

Article III. Definition.

Acts and omissions punishable by Imperial law are denominated felonies (delitos imperiales).

Felonies are committed either:

  • By means of **deceit (dolo)**, when the act is done with deliberate intent; or
  • By **fault (culpa)**, when the wrongful act results from imprudence, negligence, lack of foresight, or want of skill.
Article IV. Criminal Liability.

Criminal liability shall be incurred:

  1. By any person committing a felony, although the wrongful act done be **different from that which he intended**;
  2. By any person performing an act which would constitute an offense against person or property were it not for the inherent **impossibility of its accomplishment** or the ineffectual means employed.
Article V. Duty of the Courts.

Whenever an Imperial Court perceives an act proper to repress yet **not punishable by any law**, it shall decide the matter accordingly and submit a report to the Minister of Justice, detailing the grounds for recommending penal legislation.

Likewise, where strict application of this Code results in a clearly **excessive penalty**, the Court shall report the matter to the Minister of Justice for review, without suspending the execution of judgment.

Article VI. Degrees of Felony.

Felonies are **consummated, frustrated, or attempted**, all of which are punishable.

  • A felony is **consummated** when all elements necessary for its execution are present.
  • It is **frustrated** when all acts of execution are performed but the felony is not produced by causes independent of the perpetrator’s will.
  • It is **attempted** when the offender begins the commission by overt acts but fails to complete it due to causes other than voluntary desistance.
Article VII. When Light Felonies are Punishable.

**Light felonies** are punishable only when **consummated**, except when committed against persons or property.

Article VIII. Conspiracy and Proposal.

**Conspiracy and proposal** to commit felony are punishable only when **expressly provided by law**.

  • **Conspiracy** exists when two or more persons agree and decide to commit a felony.
  • **Proposal** exists when a person who has resolved to commit a felony proposes its execution to another.
Article IX. Classification of Felonies.
  • **Grave felonies** are those punishable by capital or afflictive penalties.
  • **Less grave felonies** are punished by correctional penalties.
  • **Light felonies** are those punishable by minor arrest or fine as set by this Code.
Article X. Offenses Under Special Imperial Laws.

Offenses punishable under special Imperial enactments are **not governed by this Code** unless expressly provided. This Code shall serve as **supplementary law** thereto.

CHAPTER TWO — Justifying and Exempting Circumstances

Article XI. Justifying Circumstances.

No criminal liability shall arise from:

  1. **Defense of one’s person or rights**, when there is:
    • Unlawful aggression;
    • Reasonable necessity of means to repel it;
    • Lack of sufficient provocation on the part of the defender.
  2. **Defense of spouse, ascendants, descendants, legitimate or adopted siblings, or relatives** within the fourth degree, under the same conditions.
  3. **Defense of a stranger**, under the first two requisites, provided the act is not driven by revenge or evil motive.
  4. The **avoidance of a greater evil**, provided:
    • The evil sought to be avoided actually exists;
    • The injury feared is greater than that done to prevent it;
    • No other less harmful means are available.
  5. **Fulfillment of a duty** or lawful exercise of right or office.
  6. **Obedience to a lawful order** issued by a superior.
Article XII. Exempting Circumstances.

The following are exempt from criminal liability:

  1. **Imbeciles or insane persons**, unless the latter act during a lucid interval.
  2. Persons **under fifteen** years old.
  3. Persons **over fifteen but under eighteen**, unless they act with discernment.
  4. Persons causing injury by pure **accident** in the performance of a lawful act with due care.
  5. Persons acting under **irresistible force**.
  6. Persons acting under **uncontrollable fear** of an equal or greater injury.
  7. Persons prevented by **lawful cause** from performing an act required by law.

CHAPTER THREE — Mitigating Circumstances

Article XIII. Mitigating Circumstances.

The following circumstances lessen criminal liability:

  1. **Incomplete justifying or exempting** circumstances;
  2. Offender **under eighteen or over seventy** years of age;
  3. **No intention** to commit so grave a wrong;
  4. **Sufficient provocation** from the offended party;
  5. Immediate **vindication of a grave offense**;
  6. Impulse of **passion or obfuscation**;
  7. **Voluntary surrender** or confession before trial;
  8. **Physical defect** restricting defense or communication;
  9. **Illness** diminishing willpower without loss of reason;
  10. Any **analogous circumstance** of equal mitigating character.

CHAPTER FOUR — Aggravating Circumstances

Article XIV. Aggravating Circumstances.

The following circumstances increase criminal liability:

  1. **Abuse of public office** or position of trust;
  2. **Contempt or insult to Imperial authorities**;
  3. **Disregard of respect** due to rank, age, or sex; or offense committed in the **dwelling** of the offended party without provocation;
  4. **Abuse of confidence** or evident ungratefulness;
  5. Crime committed within the **Imperial Palace**, in presence of the Sovereigns, or in places of worship or public duty;
  6. Crime committed **by night**, in uninhabited places, or by a **band** of armed persons;
  7. Crime committed during **conflagration, storm, epidemic**, or similar calamity;
  8. Crime committed with aid of **armed men** or with **assurance of impunity**;
  9. **Habitual delinquency or recidivism**;
  10. **Previous punishment** for equal or greater offense;
  11. Crime committed for **price, reward, or promise**;
  12. Crime committed by **destructive means** such as fire, poison, explosion, or inundation;
  13. **Evident premeditation**;
  14. Use of **craft, fraud, or disguise**;
  15. **Abuse of superior strength** or employment of means to weaken defense;
  16. **Treachery** to ensure execution without risk to oneself;
  17. Adding **ignominy** to the natural effects of the act;
  18. **Unlawful entry** by improper means;
  19. Breaking wall, roof, floor, door, or window to commit offense;
  20. Use of minors, motorized vehicles, or airships as means of offense;
  21. **Deliberate augmentation of wrong** beyond necessity.

CHAPTER FIVE — Alternative Circumstances

Article XV. Definition.

Alternative circumstances may aggravate or mitigate depending on the conditions of the act. These include:

  • **Relationship** between offender and offended party;
  • **Intoxication**;
  • Degree of **education or instruction**.

**Intoxication** is mitigating when not habitual or premeditated; otherwise, it is aggravating.

TITLE TWO: Persons Criminally Liable

Article XVI. Criminal Liability.

For grave and less grave felonies, the following are criminally liable:

  1. Principals;
  2. Accomplices;
  3. Accessories.

For light felonies, only **Principals and Accomplices** are liable.

Article XVII. Principals.

Those are principals who:

  1. Take **direct part in the execution**;
  2. **Induce others** to commit;
  3. Cooperate by **indispensable acts**.
Article XVIII. Accomplices.

Those who cooperate in the execution of the offense by **previous or simultaneous acts**, without being principals.

Article XIX. Accessories.

Those who, after knowing of the crime, participate by:

  1. **Profiting** from its effects;
  2. **Concealing evidence** or effects of the crime;
  3. **Harboring or assisting** the principal, especially in crimes against the Crown, murder, or treason.
Article XX. Exempt Accessories.

Accessories related to the principal within the degree of **spouse, ascendant, descendant, or sibling** shall not be punished, except those profiting from the crime.

TITLE THREE: Of Penalties

CHAPTER ONE — General Principles

Article XXI. Legality of Penalties.

No person shall be punished by any penalty **not prescribed by Imperial law** prior to the commission of the felony.

Article XXII. Retroactivity.

Penal laws **favorable to the offender shall apply retroactively** unless the offender is a habitual criminal.

Article XXIII. Effect of Pardon.

A pardon by the offended party **does not extinguish criminal liability**, except when the law expressly provides; but it extinguishes civil liability upon express waiver.

Article XXIV. Preventive or Safety Measures.

The following are **not considered penalties** under Imperial law:

  1. Preventive detention of accused persons;
  2. Commitment of minors to reformative institutions;
  3. Suspension from office pending trial;
  4. Administrative fines;
  5. Civil reparations as may be established by Imperial civil law.

CHAPTER TWO — Classification of Penalties

Article XXV. Imperial Scale of Penalties

Principal Penalties

  • **Capital**: Death (Imperial Execution)
  • **Afflictive**: Reclusion Perpetua, Reclusion Temporal, Absolute or Special Disqualification (Perpetual or Temporary), Prisión Mayor
  • **Correctional**: Prisión Correccional, Arresto Mayor, Suspension, or Destierro (Banishment)
  • **Light**: Arresto Menor, Public Censure
  • **Common Penalties**: Fine or Bond to Keep the Peace

Accessory Penalties

  • Disqualification (Absolute or Special)
  • Suspension of rights or offices
  • Civil interdiction
  • Indemnification
  • Forfeiture or confiscation
  • Payment of costs
